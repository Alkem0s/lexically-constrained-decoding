\begin{abstract}
Lexically constrained decoding allows users to inject domain-specific terminology into Neural Machine Translation (NMT) output at inference time, without retraining. While prior work has mostly followed structural approaches such as Grid Beam Search and Dynamic Beam Allocation, this paper takes a different route: adjusting raw output logits at each decoding step within a standard beam search pipeline. We implement and evaluate six strategies for English-to-Turkish and Turkish-to-English translation---hard exclusion, hard inclusion with dynamic anchor scheduling, soft penalty, curriculum-anchored reward, and two combined modes---using the Helsinki-NLP OPUS-MT models on an expanded bilingual test set of 500 sentence pairs. Hard exclusion and soft penalty both achieve 98.7\% constraint satisfaction with Baseline BLEU scores of 39.64 and Reference BLEU of 37.91. Soft reward with escalation reaches a Reference BLEU of 45.44 at 97.0\% satisfaction---the best quality-satisfaction trade-off among all inclusion modes. Hard inclusion achieves 72.05 Baseline BLEU at 97.6\% satisfaction while running 2.9$\times$ faster than the HuggingFace Dynamic Beam Allocation (DBA) baseline. When hard exclusion and inclusion run together, BLEU drops to 50.08 despite 95.0\% satisfaction---a non-linear degradation we term the \emph{logit squeeze effect}. We also report on challenges with subword tokenization, hyperparameter sensitivity, and exact-match enforcement in agglutinative Turkish.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% =============================================================================

Transformer-based Neural Machine Translation \cite{vaswani2017attention} achieves strong general-domain fluency, but deployed systems often require specific terminology the model will not produce on its own. A medical system must use the approved drug name; a legal translation must match jurisdiction-specific terms. Retraining for each domain is expensive, and fine-tuning risks catastrophic forgetting.

Lexically constrained decoding addresses this by enforcing vocabulary constraints at inference time, without modifying model weights. Hokamp and Liu \cite{hokamp2017lexically} introduced Grid Beam Search (GBS), which tracks constraint satisfaction across the beam. Post and Vilar \cite{post2018fast} improved efficiency with Dynamic Beam Allocation (DBA), and Hu et al.~\cite{hu2019improved} extended GBS to phrasal constraints. NeuroLogic Decoding \cite{lu2021neurologic} encodes constraints as logical predicates over the generation space.

Rather than altering the beam search structure, we explore \emph{logit-level} enforcement: directly adjusting raw output logits at each step within the standard HuggingFace Transformers pipeline \cite{wolf2020transformers}. The approach requires no changes to beam search internals and plugs into any model that exposes a LogitsProcessor interface.

This paper makes the following contributions. We implement six decoding strategies---hard exclusion, hard inclusion with dynamic anchor scheduling, soft penalty, soft curriculum reward with escalation, and two combined modes---and evaluate them on a bilingual set of 500 EN$\leftrightarrow$TR sentence pairs split into hyperparameter optimization (HPO) and held-out evaluation sets. We run a systematic search with Optuna \cite{akiba2019optuna} to find effective logit pressure settings. We benchmark against the HuggingFace DBA implementation under a morphology-aware configuration that provides it with full suffix and capitalization disjunctions, establishing a rigorous comparison point. We also document several practical issues: the \emph{logit squeeze} phenomenon in combined modes, the cost of escalation, tokenization mismatches, and the difficulty of exact-match enforcement in Turkish.

\section{Related Work}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{Structural Constrained Beam Search}

The most common approach to lexically constrained NMT is to augment beam search with an explicit constraint automaton. Hokamp and Liu \cite{hokamp2017lexically} proposed Grid Beam Search, which groups hypotheses by the number of constraints satisfied, guaranteeing that all required phrases appear in the output. Since GBS cost scales with the number of constraints, Post and Vilar \cite{post2018fast} introduced Dynamic Beam Allocation, which assigns beam slots to constraint states on demand instead of maintaining a full grid. Hu et al.~\cite{hu2019improved} further extended this to handle overlapping phrasal constraints and showed it works for monolingual text rewriting as well.

A separate line of work bakes constraint satisfaction into the training objective. Dinu et al.~\cite{dinu2019training} trained NMT models with terminology tags on the source side, teaching the model to produce the tagged term in the target. This works well but needs tagged parallel data and cannot handle new terminology at inference time.

\subsection{Logit Manipulation and Decoding Control}

Logit manipulation for controlling generation has been explored in several contexts. Anderson et al.~\cite{anderson2017guided} applied hard vocabulary restrictions in image captioning. Holtzman et al.~\cite{holtzman2020curious} showed that unconstrained beam search can produce degenerate outputs and proposed nucleus sampling as a remedy. Sha \cite{sha2020gradient} used gradient-based signals to steer decoding toward lexical targets, an idea closely related to our logit addition approach.

For free-text generation, Miao et al.~\cite{miao2019cgmh} used Metropolis-Hastings sampling for constrained sentences, and Lu et al.~\cite{lu2021neurologic} cast constraints as logical predicates solved through beam-level scoring. Unlike our work, these methods operate over completed candidate sequences rather than intervening at each step, and they do not tackle the challenges that subword tokenization and morphological inflection pose in agglutinative languages.

\subsection{Turkish NLP and Morphological Challenges}

Turkish is highly agglutinative: a single surface form can pack as much information as an entire English clause \cite{oflazer1994two}. Subword tokenization schemes like BPE \cite{sennrich2016bpe}, used by the OPUS-MT models \cite{tiedemann2020opus, tiedemann2023democratizing}, break Turkish words into multiple pieces, creating a mismatch with exact-match constraint checking. For instance, the required word ``hekim'' (physician) may naturally appear as ``hekimin'' (physician's) or ``hekime'' (to the physician), yet a naive exact-match check flags these as violations. We quantify this mismatch and discuss what it means for constraint evaluation in morphologically rich languages.

\subsection{Hyperparameter Optimization in NLP}

Automated hyperparameter search is now standard in machine learning, with frameworks like Optuna \cite{akiba2019optuna} offering efficient Bayesian optimization. In NLP, it has been used for fine-tuning schedules, learning rates, and data augmentation. We apply it to a new problem: finding the right logit pressure magnitudes, escalation thresholds, and morphological boundary penalties for constrained decoding, using a composite objective that prioritizes constraint satisfaction over translation quality.

\section{Methodology}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{System Architecture}

We build on the Helsinki-NLP OPUS-MT transformer models \cite{tiedemann2020opus, tiedemann2023democratizing}, specifically opus-mt-tc-big-en-tr and opus-mt-tc-big-tr-en, accessed through HuggingFace Transformers \cite{wolf2020transformers}. We use beam search \cite{freitag2017beam} with 16 beams, a maximum output length of 128 tokens, and no-repeat $n$-gram size of 3. Constraints are enforced through the LogitsProcessor interface, which lets us modify the raw output logits at each decoding step before beam scores are updated. The system runs in PyTorch \cite{paszke2019pytorch}.

An important preprocessing step is token expansion: each constraint word is tokenized into all plausible surface-form variants (capitalized, lowercase, with leading space, etc.). For Turkish inclusion constraints, we also enumerate common morphological suffixes to build a set of acceptable token sequences, partially addressing the agglutination mismatch discussed in Section~II-C. The overall architecture is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:architecture}.

\subsection{Hard Exclusion}

Hard exclusion prevents forbidden tokens from ever appearing in the output. At each step, the processor sets the logits of all forbidden token IDs to $-\infty$, effectively removing them from the distribution:
\begin{equation}
    l_t^{(i)} = -\infty \quad \forall\, i \in \mathcal{F},
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{F}$ is the set of forbidden token IDs and $l_t^{(i)}$ is the logit for token $i$ at step $t$. This is a simple, zero-overhead operation that guarantees 100\% exclusion satisfaction without slowing down decoding.

\subsection{Hard Inclusion with Dynamic Anchor Scheduling}

Hard inclusion forces required words to appear in the output. The main challenge is that naively boosting a required token from step 1 disrupts sentence grammar: the model gets pushed toward a word before it has built up enough context for it to fit naturally.

Our processor addresses this with a \emph{dynamic anchor schedule}. For each pending required word, the processor computes how much logit pressure to apply based on three signals: (1) whether the current step is within an ``early token'' grace period $\tau$, (2) whether the required word's natural rank in the beam is already within an acceptable threshold $R_{\text{sweet}}$, and (3) the current sentence completion ratio $\rho = t / (0.8 \cdot L_{\text{src}})$, where $L_{\text{src}}$ is the source sentence length.

Formally, at step $t$, let $r_t$ be the natural rank of the required token $w$ in the current beam, and let $l_t^{(w)}$ be its logit. The applied boost $\delta_t$ is:
\begin{equation}
\resizebox{\columnwidth}{!}{$\displaystyle
\delta_t = \begin{cases}
    0 & \text{if } t \leq \tau \quad \text{(grace period)} \\
    \beta_{\text{sweet}} & \text{if } r_t \leq R_{\text{sweet}} \quad \text{(natural fit)} \\
    \max(0,\; l_t^{*} + A_{\text{start}} + A_{\text{range}} \cdot \rho - l_t^{(w)}) & \text{otherwise,}
\end{cases}
$}
\label{eq:anchor}
\end{equation}
where $l_t^{*}$ is the maximum logit across all tokens, $A_{\text{start}}$ and $A_{\text{range}}$ are the anchor offset and range hyperparameters, and $\beta_{\text{sweet}}$ is a small buffer applied when the word is already a high-probability candidate.

An EOS-blocking mechanism also prevents the model from ending the sentence while required words are still missing, unless the output has grown too long (beyond $1.5 \times L_{\text{src}}$ tokens), at which point a safety valve kicks in to stop runaway generation. Once a required multi-token sequence is placed, a morphological boundary penalty $\gamma_{\text{suffix}}$ is applied to discourage the model from immediately suffixing the constraint word with agglutinative morphemes.

\subsection{Soft Constraint Processor}

The soft constraint processor adjusts logits continuously without making any hard guarantees. It has two components that run together.

\emph{Soft Penalty.} For each penalty token $p \in \mathcal{P}$, the processor adds a fixed negative value $\lambda_{\text{pen}} < 0$:
\begin{equation}
    l_t^{(p)} \mathrel{+}= \lambda_{\text{pen}} \quad \forall\, p \in \mathcal{P}.
\end{equation}
This reduces the probability of undesired words without eliminating them entirely, allowing the model to fall back to them if no fluent alternative exists.

\emph{Soft Curriculum Reward.} For pending reward tokens, the processor implements curriculum anchoring \cite{bengio2009curriculum}: the reward signal grows monotonically with the number of steps the reward has been active. Let $\eta$ be the number of active reward steps and $\lambda_{\text{rew}}$ be the base reward strength. The effective reward is:
\begin{equation}
    \lambda_{\text{eff}} = \min\!\left(\lambda_{\text{max}},\; \lambda_{\text{rew}} \cdot (1 + c \cdot \eta)\right),
\end{equation}
where $c$ is the curriculum growth rate and $\lambda_{\text{max}}$ caps the maximum pressure. To prevent early-token hallucination, reward application is suppressed for the first 3 decoding steps.

Rather than directly adding $\lambda_{\text{eff}}$ as a delta, the processor uses a \emph{target baseline} anchoring strategy: the reward token's logit is pulled up to $l_t^{*} + \theta_{\text{offset}} + \lambda_{\text{eff}}$ if it is below this baseline, or receives a small contextual nudge $\nu$ if it is already above it. This ensures that the required token remains competitive with the beam's top prediction without overwhelming the distribution.

\subsection{Combined Strategies}

\emph{Hard Combined.} The hard combined mode runs both exclusion and inclusion processors at the same time. One important detail is ordering: exclusion must run \emph{before} inclusion. This way, when the inclusion processor looks up the maximum logit $l_t^{*}$ for its anchor calculation (Eq.~\ref{eq:anchor}), forbidden tokens have already been masked to $-\infty$. If the order were reversed, the anchor could be calibrated against a forbidden token's high logit, causing the required word to be over-boosted.

\emph{Soft Combined.} The soft combined mode runs both penalty and reward components of SoftConstraintProcessor in a single pass, followed by a soft-boost retry pass if reward words are still missing after the first generation.

\subsection{Escalation Ladder (Soft Reward)}

The soft\_reward\_only function uses a three-tier escalation ladder: (1)~standard curriculum reward at the configured strength; (2)~maximum-strength soft boost targeting only the words still missing; (3)~hard inclusion as a last resort. The idea is to try the gentlest approach first and escalate only when needed. The downside is that difficult sentences may require up to three beam search passes.

\subsection{Hyperparameter Optimization}

We used Optuna \cite{akiba2019optuna} with the Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE) sampler to find good parameter settings. To avoid overfitting, we split the corpus into a dedicated HPO set and a held-out evaluation set.

For our proposed logit-level processors, we optimized a lexicographic composite score over 50 trials:
\begin{equation}
    \text{score}_{\text{logit}} = 1000 \cdot S + \overline{\text{BLEU}},
    \label{eq:obj}
\end{equation}
where $S \in [0, 1]$ is the constraint satisfaction rate and $\overline{\text{BLEU}}$ is the mean BLEU score relative to the unconstrained baseline.

For the DBA baseline, we set up a separate 50-trial HPO study tuning the beam size, repetition penalty, and length penalty. To directly target the baseline's tendency to repeat words, we added a penalty for length inflation to the objective:
\begin{equation}
    \text{score}_{\text{DBA}} = 1000 \cdot S + \text{BLEU} - \max(0, R_{\text{len}} - 1.3) \times 200,
    \label{eq:obj_dba}
\end{equation}
where $R_{\text{len}}$ is the average output length ratio. This composite score guides Optuna to find settings that terminate generation naturally. All optimized hyperparameters are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:hpo_params}.

\section{Experimental Setup}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{Models and Hardware}

All experiments use the Helsinki-NLP OPUS-MT ``big'' transformer models: opus-mt-tc-big-en-tr for English-to-Turkish and opus-mt-tc-big-tr-en for Turkish-to-English \cite{tiedemann2020opus, tiedemann2023democratizing}. These are 6-layer encoder-decoder transformers with 8 attention heads and a model dimension of 512. Models are downloaded and cached locally. We ran everything on a single NVIDIA GPU with CUDA support, using PyTorch \cite{paszke2019pytorch} in no-gradient inference mode.

\subsection{Test Set}

We assembled a bilingual evaluation set of 500 sentence pairs: 250 with English sources and Turkish targets, and 250 the other way around. The sentences span domains including medicine, technology, economics, climate science, linguistics, and everyday life, and were deliberately chosen so that constraints involve plausible synonyms or domain-specific terminology the unconstrained model would not naturally produce. To avoid overfitting hyperparameters to the evaluation distribution, sentences were split into a separate HPO set (used only during Optuna search) and a held-out evaluation set (used for final metrics).

Each test case specifies: (1) \emph{forbidden words} for hard and soft exclusion; (2) \emph{required words} for hard and soft inclusion; (3) \emph{penalty words} (forbidden words used in soft modes); (4) \emph{reward words} (required words used in soft modes). The unconstrained baseline translation is computed once per sentence before applying any constraint mode. Note that not all modes operate over the full 500 sentences: exclusion-only modes are evaluated on all 305 exclusion-annotated cases ($N=305$), while inclusion-bearing and combined modes are evaluated on the 497 cases carrying required-word annotations ($N=497$).

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

We measure each mode on four fronts. \emph{Constraint Satisfaction Rate} ($S$): the fraction of test cases where all required words appear in the output (inclusion) and no forbidden words appear (exclusion), checked with a morphology-aware matcher that accepts valid Turkish inflectional suffixes. \emph{Baseline BLEU}: computed against the unconstrained baseline output, so it captures how much the constraint changes the output rather than absolute quality---a Baseline BLEU of 100 means the constraint had no effect. \emph{Reference BLEU} \cite{papineni2002bleu} and \emph{ChrF} \cite{popovic2015chrf}: both computed against human ground-truth reference translations, measuring absolute translation quality. \emph{Length Ratio}: average constrained-to-unconstrained output length, which serves as a quick diagnostic for word-appending artifacts.

\subsection{Ablation Design}

The hard\_inclusion\_ablation mode is identical to hard\_inclusion under the hood, and was included to isolate the effect of the dynamic anchoring schedule versus the suffix penalty. Since the code path is the same, the results match, serving as a sanity check on the evaluation pipeline.

\subsection{Hard Constraint and DBA Baseline Analysis}

Hard exclusion achieves high satisfaction (98.7\%) with a mean BLEU of 39.64 relative to the unconstrained baseline and a Reference BLEU of 37.91, with a length ratio of 1.012. The slight elongation indicates that the model rephrases to avoid forbidden terms without significant length inflation.

Hard inclusion achieves 97.6\% satisfaction across all 497 inclusion-annotated cases, with a Baseline BLEU of 72.05 and a length ratio of 1.109. The remaining unsatisfied cases involve morphologically complex Turkish targets where no vocabulary token matches the required surface form.

We benchmark against the HuggingFace DBA baseline evaluated in a morphology-aware configuration: \texttt{DisjunctiveConstraint} is used to encompass all suffix and capitalization variants for required words, and a complete \texttt{flat\_token\_ids} map is provided for \texttt{bad\_words\_ids}. Despite this favorable setup, the DBA baseline achieves a constraint satisfaction rate of 96.2\% with an average latency of 2022.6~ms per sentence---nearly 2.9$\times$ slower than our hard inclusion (700.2~ms). Furthermore, the DBA baseline exhibits a substantial length ratio of 2.187, indicating severe output inflation. Qualitative inspection reveals that the structural beam allocation mechanism in DBA frequently gets trapped in repetitive punctuation and subword babbling loops (e.g., generating strings like \emph{``...değil mi?.. b...???!??.? ???...??''}) as it forces hypotheses into constraint-satisfying paths. Fig.~\ref{fig:length_ratio} makes the length inflation contrast visible across all modes, and Fig.~\ref{fig:latency} compares decoding latency directly.

\subsection{The Logit Squeeze Effect in Hard Combined}

Hard combined enforces both exclusion and inclusion simultaneously. The results show 95.0\% satisfaction with a Baseline BLEU of 50.08 and a length ratio of 1.107. Despite the improved dynamic anchoring, the combined mode still falls below both isolated modes. When exclusion masks forbidden tokens to $-\infty$, the inclusion processor then anchors against that reduced distribution. The two constraints interact: the distribution is narrowed from one side and pulled from another. We refer to this as the \emph{logit squeeze effect}.

Logit-level hard constraints do not compose linearly. The individual BLEU scores (39.64 for exclusion, 72.05 for inclusion) would suggest a combined score somewhere in that range; instead we get 50.08. Fig.~\ref{fig:squeeze_plot} shows this gap.

\subsection{Soft Constraint Analysis}

Soft penalty achieves high satisfaction (98.7\%) with a Baseline BLEU of 39.64 and a length ratio of 1.012, matching hard exclusion on both satisfaction and BLEU while avoiding any hard masking. The results suggest that soft penalties can preserve fluency while still discouraging forbidden tokens.

Soft reward with escalation delivers the highest BLEU preservation among all modes, at 80.00 Baseline BLEU and 45.44 Reference BLEU, with a satisfaction rate of 97.0\% (nearly matching hard inclusion). Its ChrF of 71.95 exceeds the unconstrained baseline of 67.02, meaning the constrained output remains close to human reference quality. The escalation ladder activates on 186 out of 497 cases; the majority of sentences are handled on the first soft pass. The remaining unsatisfied cases involve required words that cannot be naturally introduced even under maximum soft pressure, with hard inclusion also failing---typically due to subword vocabulary gaps.

Soft combined achieves 66.0\% satisfaction with a Baseline BLEU of 62.30 and 170 escalations. With penalty and reward active simultaneously, the model faces opposing pressures. In about a third of cases the two pull in incompatible directions, and satisfaction drops accordingly. Soft reward alone reaches 97.0\%; adding penalties costs roughly 31.0 percentage points. Fig.~\ref{fig:ref_quality} shows Reference BLEU and ChrF across all modes relative to the unconstrained baseline and DBA.

\subsection{Qualitative Analysis of Morphological Failures}

A qualitative review of the failed cases (out of 497) in Hard Inclusion highlights core morphological and alignment limitations. In the source sentence \emph{``The international space station orbits the Earth''}, the required words were \emph{``uzay istasyonu''} (space station) and \emph{``yörünge''} (orbit). The model generated \emph{``uzay istasyonu''} twice but failed to output \emph{``yörünge''}: the two anchor climb schedules competed, and the model settled on repeating the more probable phrase rather than producing both targets.

A second failure arises from semantic mismatch. In \emph{``Film izlemeyi sever''} with required target word \emph{``cinema''}, the baseline model strongly preferred the synonym \emph{``movies''}. The dynamic anchor was unable to push \emph{``cinema''} above the baseline token probability because structural syntax templates locked in the alternative from the first decoding step.

The third failure involves ungrammatical insertion. In \emph{``Küçük köpek büyük kediye havladı''} with required words \emph{``tiny''} and \emph{``feline''}, the model generated \emph{``The little dog tiny barked at the big cat''}, placing \emph{``tiny''} in an ungrammatical slot and never producing \emph{``feline''}. This case illustrates that applying simultaneous logit pressure for multiple tokens can disrupt target syntax coherence when the required words compete for the same structural position.

\subsection{HPO Convergence and Sensitivity}

The Optuna search settles on a stable region within approximately 10 trials: once maximum satisfaction is reached, the BLEU component selects the configuration that best preserves translation quality. Expanding the search space to allow positive \emph{Anchor Offset} values produced configurations with significantly lower BLEU, consistent with over-anchoring being detrimental. The final parameters constrain the anchor offset to $[-10, 0]$, penalize forbidden tokens with up to $-49.27$ logit reduction, and use a relative \emph{Inclusion Boost} of up to $9.16$ above max-logit. \emph{Anchor Start} and \emph{Early Token Grace Period} show the highest sensitivity: large shifts in either degrade satisfaction or BLEU by more than 5 percentage points on the HPO set. For the DBA baseline, Optuna converges to a beam size of 6 to 8, a repetition penalty of 1.08 to 1.28, and a length penalty of -0.67 to 0.58, which helps balance constraint satisfaction against length inflation and repetition loops.

\subsection{Turkish Morphology and Constraint Mismatch}

Looking at the output transcripts, word-appending artifacts in Turkish consistently stem from the agglutination mismatch. When the constraint is a bare root like ``hekim'' (physician) but the model naturally prefers ``hekimin'' (physician-GEN) or ``hekime'' (physician-DAT), the satisfaction check fails and EOS-blocking forces the bare root to be appended at the end. Our morphological suffix expansion covers common inflections but cannot enumerate every possible Turkish suffix chain. The suffix penalty parameter (\emph{Suffix Penalty TR}) helps by discouraging the model from immediately inflecting a freshly placed constraint word before the satisfaction check can confirm it.

\subsection{Methodological Transparency}

We note three main limitations. First, while the corpus is split into HPO and evaluation sets, both are drawn from the same curated bilingual source, so some distributional overlap remains. Second, the soft reward mode's strong BLEU results require up to three beam search passes per sentence, which may not be practical for latency-sensitive applications. Third, while Baseline BLEU captures constraint-induced output change precisely, Reference BLEU and ChrF are computed against a single human reference per sentence and may not fully reflect output quality for sentences with many equally valid translations.

% =============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
% =============================================================================

We evaluated six approaches to logit-level lexical constraint enforcement for English--Turkish NMT using the OPUS-MT models on a 500-sentence bilingual evaluation set. Several key trends emerged. Soft constraints produce more fluent translations than hard ones, and when combined with a multi-tier escalation ladder, they achieve the highest overall translation quality---80.00 Baseline BLEU and 45.44 Reference BLEU (ChrF 71.95)---while maintaining a 97.0\% satisfaction rate. Running hard exclusion and inclusion together produces a \emph{logit squeeze effect}: quality drops to 50.08 BLEU despite 95.0\% satisfaction, below what either mode achieves in isolation.

We benchmarked against the HuggingFace DBA baseline under a rigorous morphology-aware configuration and demonstrated that, despite its structural constraint guarantees, DBA suffers from severe length inflation (length ratio 2.187), lower translation quality (Reference BLEU 44.68 vs.\ 45.44 for soft reward), and substantially higher latency (2022.6~ms vs.\ 644.6~ms). Logit-level dynamic anchoring and curriculum reward maintain syntactic fluency while running under 705~ms per sentence.0~ms per sentence.

There is room to develop morphology-aware constraint metrics for agglutinative languages, to explore joint training of constraint processors, and to investigate differentiable logit manipulation optimized end-to-end during inference. The dynamic anchor scheduling, relative logit boosting, and curriculum reward ideas explored here are not specific to Turkish or OPUS-MT, though how well they generalize to other language pairs and model families remains to be tested.
